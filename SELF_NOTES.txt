# What is a Satellite?
    A satellite is any object that orbits (revolves around) another, larger object due to gravity.

    There are two main types:

    Natural satellites – These occur naturally in space.
    Example: Earth's Moon is a natural satellite because it orbits Earth.

    Artificial satellites – These are human-made objects launched into space.
    They orbit Earth or other planets and are used for purposes such as:
        Communication (TV, phone, internet)
        Weather forecasting
        Navigation (such as GPS)
        Scientific research
        Earth observation and mapping


# What is space debris?
    Space debris (also called space junk) is any non-functional, human-made object left in space, especially in Earth's orbit.
    Even very small pieces of debris travel at extremely high speeds (around 28,000 km/h or 17,500 mph) in low Earth orbit. At these speeds, 
    even a tiny fragment can:
    
    Damage or destroy satellites
    Endanger astronauts and spacecraft
    Create even more debris through collisions (called the Kessler syndrome)


# Types of Earth orbits
    Low Earth Orbit (LEO): About 160–2,000 km above Earth. Used by the International Space Station and many Earth-observation satellites.
   
    Medium Earth Orbit (MEO): About 2,000–35,786 km. Used by navigation satellites like GPS.
    
    Geostationary Orbit (GEO): About 35,786 km above the equator. Satellites here appear fixed over one point on Earth and are 
    commonly used for communication and weather monitoring.

# A TLE (Two-Line Element) file 
    is a text file that contains the orbital parameters of an Earth-orbiting satellite. These parameters are used to  calculate the satellite's position and predict where it will be at any time.
    Each satellite's orbital data is stored in two lines of text (plus an optional name line).

    ISS (ZARYA)
    1 25544U 98067A   26198.50000000  .00016717  00000-0  10270-3 0  9993
    2 25544  51.6400 123.4567 0003456 234.5678 125.4321 15.50012345678901

    Line 1: Satellite identification and other orbital information
    Line 2: Orbital elements

# A TLE includes important orbital parameters such as:

    Satellite number
    Epoch (date and time the data is valid)
    Inclination (tilt of the orbit)
    Right Ascension of the Ascending Node (RAAN)
    Eccentricity (shape of the orbit)
    Argument of Perigee
    Mean Anomaly
    Mean Motion (number of orbits per day)

# TLE files are used to:

    Predict a satellite's position.
    Track satellites from Earth.
    Plan satellite observations.
    Avoid collisions in space.
    Simulate satellite orbits.
